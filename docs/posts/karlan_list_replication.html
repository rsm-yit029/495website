Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Replicating Karlan & List (2007)

replication
field experiment
econometrics
A classic field experiment shows matching grants boost charitable giving — but bigger match ratios don’t actually help.
Author

Ria Tang

Published

April 21, 2026

Introduction

Every year, Americans donate more than 2% of GDP to charitable causes, with over 89% of households giving to at least one organization. Yet despite the scale of this market, fundraisers have long relied on intuition rather than evidence. One of the most widespread beliefs in the industry is that larger matching ratios are more powerful: a “$3 for every $1 you give” offer is assumed to be far more compelling than a simple 1:1 match.

Dean Karlan and John A. List put this assumption to a rigorous test in their 2007 American Economic Review paper, “Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Evidence from a Large-Scale Natural Field Experiment.”

What is the “price” of charitable giving? When a leadership donor offers to match contributions at a 2:1 ratio, a donor effectively pays $1 to deliver $3 to the charity — the implicit price of providing $1 of the public good falls from $1.00 to $0.33. Standard economic theory predicts that lower prices increase demand, so higher match ratios should increase donations.

To test this, the authors partnered with a U.S. liberal nonprofit and mailed solicitation letters to 50,083 prior donors in August 2005. Individuals were randomly assigned to either a control group (no match offer) or one of several treatment conditions varying along three dimensions: the match ratio (1:1, 2:1, or 3:1), the maximum size of the matching fund ($25K, $50K, $100K, or unstated), and the suggested donation amount.

The results were striking — and largely contrary to fundraising conventional wisdom:

  • Matching works: the match offer increased the response rate by about 22% and revenue per solicitation by roughly 19% compared to the control group.
  • But larger ratios do not help: the 2:1 and 3:1 treatments produced virtually identical results to the 1:1 treatment — the difference in match ratio had no statistically significant effect.
  • Political context matters: the match effect appears to be driven mainly by donors in states that voted for George W. Bush in 2004, with little evidence of an effect in blue states.

These findings have direct practical implications: organizations spending resources to secure higher match ratios may be wasting money. The mere existence of a match — not its size — is what moves donors.

In the sections below, we replicate the paper’s key findings using the publicly available dataset. We reproduce Table 1 (balance checks confirming successful randomization), Table 2A Panel A (the main treatment effects on response rate and donation amount), and Table 3 Column 1 (the regression estimates of the match effect on the probability of giving). We also extend the paper’s results by visualizing the red/blue state heterogeneity, which the authors present only in tabular form.

Setup

Replication 1: Table 1 — Balance Check

A successful randomization means that the treatment and control groups should look statistically identical on all pre-treatment observable characteristics. Table 1 in the paper reports means for both groups across a set of baseline variables. If randomization worked, none of the differences should be statistically significant.

Replication 2: Table 2A (Panel A) — Main Treatment Effects

Table 2A reports three outcome measures across treatment conditions: the response rate (fraction of recipients who donated), the unconditional average donation (including zeros), and the conditional average donation (among givers only). Panel A covers the full sample.

def group_stats(mask, label):
    sub = df[mask]
    gave_m  = sub['gave'].mean()
    gave_se = sub['gave'].sem()
    amt_m   = sub['amount'].mean()
    amt_se  = sub['amount'].sem()
    cond    = sub.loc[sub['gave'] == 1, 'amount']
    cond_m  = cond.mean()
    cond_se = cond.sem()
    return {
        'Group':              label,
        'N':                  f"{len(sub):,}",
        'Response Rate':      f"{gave_m:.3f} ({gave_se:.3f})",
        'Amount Uncond. ($)': f"{amt_m:.3f} ({amt_se:.3f})",
        'Amount Cond. ($)':   f"{cond_m:.3f} ({cond_se:.3f})",

Replication 3: Table 3 (Columns 1–2) — Regression Estimates

The paper labels Table 3 as a probit regression, but as noted in the assignment, the reported coefficients closely resemble OLS linear probability model estimates. We use OLS here, which faithfully reproduces the published numbers.

We estimate two specifications:

  • Column 1: gave ~ treatment (simple treatment indicator only)
  • Column 2: gave ~ treatment interacted with ratio, threshold, and ask-amount dummies
Show code 
m1 = smf.ols('gave ~ treatment', data=df).fit(cov_type='HC1')

m2 = smf.ols(
    'gave ~ treatment'
    ' + treatment:ratio2 + treatment:ratio3'
    ' + treatment:size25 + treatment:size50 + treatment:size100'
    ' + treatment:askd2  + treatment:askd3',
    data=df
).fit(cov_type='HC1')

def fmt(model, var):
    if var not in model.params:
        return '---', '---'
    c, se, p = model.params[var], model.bse[var], model.pvalues[var]
    stars = '***' if p < 0.01 else '**' if p < 0.05 else '*' if p < 0.1 else ''
    return f"{c:.3f}{stars}", f"({se:.3f})"

rows = []
for var, label in [
    ('treatment',         'Treatment'),
    ('treatment:ratio2',  'Treatment x 2:1 ratio'),
    ('treatment:ratio3',  'Treatment x 3:1 ratio'),
    ('treatment:size25',  'Treatment x $25K threshold'),
    ('treatment:size50',  'Treatment x $50K threshold'),
    ('treatment:size100', 'Treatment x $100K threshold'),
    ('treatment:askd2',   'Treatment x medium ask'),
    ('treatment:askd3',   'Treatment x high ask'),
    ('Intercept',         'Constant'),
]:
    c1, s1 = fmt(m1, var)
    c2, s2 = fmt(m2, var)
    rows.append({'Variable': label, 'Col (1)': c1, 'SE': s1, 'Col (2)': c2, 'SE ': s2})

t3 = pd.DataFrame(rows)
print(t3.to_string(index=False))

Interpretation: The treatment coefficient in Column 1 is 0.004 and highly significant, matching the value reported in Table 3 Column 1 of the paper. This implies that receiving a match offer raises the probability of donating by approximately 0.4 percentage points off a baseline rate of 1.8% — a relative increase of around 22%. In Column 2, the interaction terms for the 2:1 and 3:1 ratios are small and statistically insignificant, consistent with the paper’s finding that larger ratios add nothing beyond the 1:1 baseline. The sub-treatment interactions for threshold size and ask amount are similarly close to zero.

Additional Analysis: Visualizing the Red/Blue State Heterogeneity

The paper’s most striking result — that the match effect appears to be driven mainly by red-state donors — is reported in Table 5 as regression coefficients across Bush vote-share bins. Here we visualize it directly, making the geographic heterogeneity immediately apparent.

Show code 
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 5))
fig.suptitle('The Match Effect Depends on Political Environment',
             fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')

for ax, (title, state_var) in zip(axes, [('Blue States', 'blue0'), ('Red States', 'red0')]):
    sub        = df[df[state_var] == 1]
    ctrl_rate  = sub.loc[sub['control']   == 1, 'gave'].mean()
    treat_rate = sub.loc[sub['treatment'] == 1, 'gave'].mean()

    bars = ax.bar(['Control', 'Treatment'], [ctrl_rate, treat_rate],
                  color=['#5B8DB8', '#E07B54'], width=0.45,
                  edgecolor='white', linewidth=1.5)

    for bar, val in zip(bars, [ctrl_rate, treat_rate]):
        ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
                bar.get_height() + 0.0002,
                f'{val:.1%}', ha='center', va='bottom',
                fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')

    diff  = treat_rate - ctrl_rate
    color = '#C0392B' if diff > 0.005 else '#7F8C8D'
    ax.text(0.5, 0.88, f'delta = {diff:+.1%}',
            transform=ax.transAxes, ha='center', fontsize=12,
            color=color, fontweight='bold',
            bbox=dict(boxstyle='round,pad=0.3', facecolor='white',
                      edgecolor=color, alpha=0.8))

    ax.set_title(title, fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_ylabel('Response Rate', fontsize=10)
    ax.set_ylim(0, max(ctrl_rate, treat_rate) * 1.4)
    ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(
        plt.FuncFormatter(lambda x, _: f'{x:.1%}'))
    ax.spines[['top', 'right']].set_visible(False)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('red_blue_effect.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
plt.show()

Interpretation: In blue states, the treatment and control response rates are close to one another, suggesting little evidence of a match effect among donors who already strongly support the organization’s liberal mission. In red states, the gap between treatment and control is noticeably larger, consistent with the paper’s finding that the match effect is concentrated among red-state donors. The authors suggest this may reflect a signaling mechanism: for donors living in a politically minority environment, the announcement of a matching gift may serve as a credibility or timing signal that makes the ask feel more urgent and legitimate.

Conclusion

Karlan & List (2007) deliver two counter-intuitive but robust results. First, matching grants work: merely announcing that a leadership donor will match contributions raises response rates and revenue per solicitation. Second, and more surprisingly, the size of the match ratio appears not to matter — donors respond just as strongly to a 1:1 match as to a 3:1 match.

Our replication closely reproduces the paper’s key numbers. The balance checks confirm the randomization was successful. Table 2A shows the response rate rising from 1.8% in the control group to 2.2% in the treatment group, with the three ratio sub-groups remaining indistinguishable from one another. The OLS regression in Table 3 recovers a treatment coefficient of 0.004, significant at the 1% level, with the ratio interaction terms close to zero and insignificant.

The additional visualization makes the red/blue state heterogeneity tangible: the aggregate match effect appears to be driven mainly by red-state donors, with little evidence of an effect in blue states. This suggests charitable giving is shaped not only by the price of the public good, but also by social identity and political context — a finding with broad implications for how organizations design and target their fundraising campaigns.
